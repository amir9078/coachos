01 — The pitch

Everything a coach's business needs, in one desk

122,974 professional coaches generate $5.34B a year, yet only 19% invested in any new technology last year while 54% call better platforms a priority. Today a coach stitches together 5–8 disconnected tools. CoachOS replaces the stitching — one desk, every room of the business, AI built into each workflow, at solo-coach pricing where no incumbent has built AI-first.

$5.34BGlobal coaching industry, +17% YoY
122,974Coaches worldwide
54%Want better tech — only 19% bought any
$29–59/mo target price, matching category norm

Positioning: "You coach. CoachOS runs everything else." We are the coach's infrastructure, not their replacement — which keeps the 122,974 coaches as allies, not disruptees, and keeps us in the EU AI Act's lighter limited-risk tier.

The gap: nobody in the $20–60/mo tier is AI-first around the coach's actual daily workflow. Enterprise players won't come down-market; solo-tier incumbents haven't rebuilt around AI.

Defensibility: model-capability advantages last ~6 months (Jasper fell from ~$120M to $35–55M revenue when its wrapper edge evaporated). Our moats, in order: (1) accumulated client history + contracts + pipeline inside the platform, (2) coach-community distribution, (3) the human-service layer.

Regulation

Assistive AI sits in the EU AI Act's limited-risk tier — disclose "this is AI," largely compliant. Lines never crossed: no employee-evaluation features, no emotion/sentiment scoring. GDPR: coach is data controller, we are processor — standard DPA before first EU customer. Independent security review is non-negotiable before real client data (a vibe-coded competitor product was publicly breached post-launch).

Rejected claims (do not use): "$62B AI coaching market by 2026" (contradicts verified industry size 10×, uncited); "$5.48B AI Career Coach market, $14.82B by 2030" (no disclosed methodology); "$3.64B life coaching market" (inconsistent with primary data); "25% of YC W25 was 95% AI-generated code" (failed verification).

Coach directory models — two different businesses

Noomii runs a pure listing model: free basic profile, ~$449/yr premium, no commission — the coaching deal happens off-platform. BetterUp runs a managed marketplace: coaches are vetted and AI-matched, and the platform takes ~30–40% of session fees (general marketplace commissions run 15–40%). CoachOS follows Noomii's model — a commission marketplace would make us the merchant of record and effectively the coach's agency, contradicting the whole plan's thesis that we're infrastructure, not competition. Full reasoning: doc 01 §6.

03 — Product spec

Seven modules, one platform

Module-by-module: what the coach gets, what's AI, and what stays human.

AI

1–2. Leads

Landing pages, lead scoring, pipeline board, AI follow-up nudges. AI drafts — coach always sends.

AI + Human

3. Marketing

Weekly posts + newsletter drafted in the coach's voice; optional done-for-you service.

4. Bookings

Scheduling, calendar sync, reminders, no-show recovery — via Cal.com, not hand-built.

5. Contracts & Payments

Coaching agreement templates, e-sign, invoicing, subscriptions.

AI · The wedge

6. Client Delivery

Notes → AI summary + action items, progress timeline, prep briefs. Built first.

AI

7. Practice Intelligence

Revenue, retention, at-risk clients flagged by AI — insights to the coach only.

8. Automations

Pre-built recipes the coach toggles on — n8n-backed, never a workflow builder.

New channel

9. Coach & Mentor Directory

Public opt-in profile; enquiries route straight into the coach's own Leads pipeline. Listing-based, never a commission.

10. Daily Briefing

News by niche category, a world catchup, and a 5-minute learn — one shared digest per category, not per coach. Public on the website too.

Plus one cross-cutting capability: Gmail integration (Phase 2) — approved sends go out from the coach's own address so replies land in their real inbox, not a CoachOS address.

04 — Engineering

Build vs. integrate — the open-source map

We do not hand-build commodity modules. Proven open-source engines exist for scheduling, e-sign, CRM and email — we integrate them as separate services (not code-merged, mostly for AGPL licensing reasons) and concentrate our own code on the AI workflows that differentiate us.

What we build ourselves — the actual moat: the AI workflow layer (notes→summary, prep briefs, voice-profile content, lead scoring), the unified dashboard, the client portal, and the approval-first AI UX.

Phase-1 reality check: the wedge needs only Next.js + Supabase + Claude API + a Cal.com embed. Everything else arrives later — standing up five services on day one is an operational liability before revenue justifies it.

05 — Execution

Phased roadmap, hard gates

Each phase has an explicit gate. The next phase does not start until the gate is passed — the discipline that separates this plan from the all-in-one graveyard.

1

The wedge

Client Delivery + scheduling embed + portal. Months 1–3.

Gate: 5 pilots weekly, 3 paid
2

Gate: 25 paying, churn <5%

Growth loop

4

Intelligence

Practice dashboard, at-risk AI, referrals. Year 2.

Gate: earned, not assumed

Costs (verified unit economics)

Build to first paying customer: ~$1,000–2,500 (hosting, security review, legal templates). Runtime AI cost on Claude Haiku 4.5: ~$0.03/coach/month — 300 coaches ≈ $8.40/mo. At $59/mo revenue per coach, gross margin exceeds 95%. AI cost never drives architecture decisions here — the founder's selling time is the scarce resource, not compute.

Gmail, Automations (n8n-backed) and Directory search (Supabase full-text) add close to $0 in new infrastructure cost — the real new-line-item risk is Google's app-verification review for Gmail sending, which can take several weeks and should start in Phase 2, month 4, in parallel with the build.

Go-to-market

Sales-led, zero ad spend to start: recruit 5 pilots from ICF chapters and coach communities → convert + ask for 2 introductions each (coaches refer coaches) → ICF workshops + certification-program partnerships → done-for-you service doubles as a retention and referral channel.

06 — For the builder

Building it with Claude Code + Cursor

Claude Code builds features end-to-end from plain-English briefs; Cursor is the review-and-touch-up surface. Stack: Next.js + TypeScript + Tailwind + Supabase (auth + Postgres + row-level security) + Claude API (server-side, Haiku 4.5 default) + Cal.com + Vercel.

Functionality build order (Phase 1)

  1. F0 — Scaffold.

    Next.js + Supabase auth + folder structure + design tokens.

  2. F1 — Clients CRUD.

    Add/edit clients, engagements, goals — RLS isolation tested with two accounts.

  3. F2 — Notes → AI summary.

    Paste notes → editable draft (client-facing, coach-facing, action items). Nothing sent yet.

  4. F3 — Approval queue + send.

    Dashboard queue; Approve emails the client and logs to audit_log.

  5. F4 — Progress timeline.

    Goals + session-by-session arc, fed by approved summaries.

  6. F5 — Prep brief.

    30-minutes-before brief on commitments, blockers, stalled goals.

  7. F6 — Bookings.

    Cal.com embed, webhook sync to sessions table.

  8. F7 — Client portal.

    Read-only, signed-token link — no login friction.

  9. F8 — Niche packs v1.

    Pack picker swaps tone/templates.

  10. F9 — Polish + security gate.

    Landing page, empty/loading states, then independent security review before real client data.

Phase 2 continues (F10–F14): contracts, Stripe billing, and Gmail OAuth connect — start Google's verification review the same week, not after building. Phase 3 continues (F15–F20): leads pipeline, marketing composer, Automations v1 (3–5 n8n-backed recipes), the Coach & Mentor Directory — built, but held unlaunched until coach supply clears the 100-paying-coach gate — Daily Briefing, a per-category news/world-catchup/5-minute-learn digest generated once a day and shared across all coaches in that niche, and an admin MCP server (F20) that lets the team push, pull, and update briefing content — news, images, corrections — directly from a Claude conversation, running locally with its own service-role credential, never handed to coaches. Full detail: docs/05-build-guide.md.

Example brief for F2

"Build the summarize endpoint + client-detail notes flow: paste notes → Summarize → editable draft (client-facing summary, coach-facing note, action items), using the prompt template in docs/05 §4.3. RLS on. Nothing sends until approved."

Hard rules baked into CLAUDE.md/.cursorrules: RLS on every table; all Claude API calls server-side only; outbound artifacts require status='approved' + audit log; AI content labeled "AI-drafted, coach-approved"; no emotion-scoring or employee-evaluation features, ever.
